## 35.5-1

> Prove equation $\text{(35.23)}$. Then show that after executing line 5 of $\text{EXACT-SUBSET-SUM}$, $L_i$ is a sorted list containing every element of $P_i$ whose value is not more than $t$.



For $P_i$ (all subset sums using first $i$ items), each subset either excludes $x_i$ or includes it. Therefore
$$
P_i=P_{i-1}\cup\{y+x_i:y\in P_{i-1}\},
$$
which is equation (35.23).

In $\text{EXACT-SUBSET-SUM}$, line 3 forms
$$
M_i=\text{MERGE-LISTS}(L_{i-1},L_{i-1}+x_i),
$$
so $M_i$ is sorted and contains exactly $P_i$ (by induction from $L_{i-1}$). Line 5 deletes all values $>t$ from this sorted list, preserving order. Hence after line 5, $L_i$ is sorted and contains exactly those elements of $P_i$ that are at most $t$.

## 35.5-2

> Using induction on $i$, prove inequality $\text{(35.26)}$.



Let $\delta=\varepsilon/(2n)$ (the TRIM parameter).  
Claim (35.26): for every $y\in P_i$, there exists $z\in L_i$ such that
$$
z\le y\le (1+\delta)^i z.
$$

Proof by induction on $i$.

Base $i=0$: $P_0=L_0=\{0\}$, trivial.

Inductive step: take $y\in P_i$. By (35.23), either $y\in P_{i-1}$ or $y=y'+x_i$ with $y'\in P_{i-1}$. By induction, some $z'\in L_{i-1}$ satisfies
$$
z'\le y' \le (1+\delta)^{i-1}z'
$$
(or $y'=y$ in the first case). Thus before trimming, $M_i$ contains an element $m$ with
$$
m\le y\le (1+\delta)^{i-1}m.
$$
TRIM guarantees for each $m\in M_i$ a retained $z\in L_i$ with
$$
z\le m\le (1+\delta)z.
$$
Combining,
$$
z\le y\le (1+\delta)^i z.
$$
So (35.26) holds.

## 35.5-3

> Prove inequality $\text{(35.29)}$.



Using (35.26) with $i=n$ and $y=y^\*$ (optimal feasible value), there exists $z\in L_n$ such that
$$
z\le y^\*\le (1+\delta)^n z,\qquad \delta=\frac{\varepsilon}{2n}.
$$
Hence
$$
z\ge \frac{y^\*}{(1+\varepsilon/(2n))^n}.
$$
Now use
$$
\left(1+\frac{\varepsilon}{2n}\right)^n\le e^{\varepsilon/2}\le 1+\varepsilon
\quad(0<\varepsilon\le1),
$$
which is inequality (35.29). Therefore
$$
z\ge \frac{y^\*}{1+\varepsilon}\ge (1-\varepsilon)y^\*.
$$
This is the required bound in the approximation proof.

## 35.5-4

> How would you modify the approximation scheme presented in this section to ﬁnd a good approximation to the smallest value not less than $t$ that is a sum of some subset of the given input list?



Let $T=\sum_{x\in S}x$. Seeking the smallest subset-sum at least $t$ is equivalent to seeking the largest subset-sum at most $T-t$ for the complement:
if subset $A$ has sum $a\ge t$, then complement $B=S\setminus A$ has sum $b=T-a\le T-t$, and minimizing $a$ is equivalent to maximizing $b$.

So run $\text{APPROX-SUBSET-SUM}(S,\ T-t,\ \varepsilon)$ to get an approximate maximum $b^\*$, then return
$$
a^\*=T-b^\*.
$$
This gives a correspondingly good approximation to the minimum feasible value above $t$.

## 35.5-5

> Modify the $\text{APPROX-SUBSET-SUM}$ procedure to also return the subset of $S$ that sums to the value $z^\*$.



Store each list entry as a record
$$
(\text{value},\ \text{prev-pointer},\ \text{picked-index}),
$$
where `prev-pointer` links to the record in $L_{i-1}$ from which it came, and `picked-index` is either $i$ (if created from $y+x_i$) or `nil` (if copied from $y$).

During MERGE and TRIM, when retaining a value, retain its associated record (pointer metadata). If duplicates are merged, keep any one representative record.

At the end, let the chosen output value be $z^\*\in L_n$. Recover the subset by following pointers backward from its record to $L_0$, collecting every non-`nil` `picked-index`. Those indices are exactly the elements of $S$ summing to $z^\*$.
