## 34.4-1

> Consider the straightforward (nonpolynomial-time) reduction in the proof of Theorem 34.9. Describe a circuit of size $n$ that, when converted to a formula by this method, yields a formula whose size is exponential in $n$.

Let $g_1 = x$, and for $i \ge 1$ let $g_{i+1} = g_i \wedge g_i$. As a circuit, this has size $O(n)$, because each new gate just takes two wires from the previous gate.

But the straightforward conversion to a formula duplicates the formula for $g_i$ twice when forming the formula for $g_{i+1}$. Hence the resulting formula size satisfies $s_{i+1} = 2 s_i + O(1)$, so $s_i = 2^{\Theta(i)}$. Therefore a size-$n$ circuit can yield an exponential-size formula.

## 34.4-2

> Show the $\text{3-CNF}$ formula that results when we use the method of Theorem 34.10 on the formula $\text{(34.3)}$.

Using the subformula variables $y_1,\dots,y_6$ from the proof, and extra variables $p,q$ to pad short clauses, the resulting $\text{3-CNF}$ formula is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\phi ={}&
(y_1 \vee p \vee q)
\wedge (y_1 \vee p \vee \neg q)
\wedge (y_1 \vee \neg p \vee q)
\wedge (y_1 \vee \neg p \vee \neg q) \\
&\wedge (\neg y_1 \vee \neg y_2 \vee \neg x_2)
\wedge (\neg y_1 \vee y_2 \vee \neg x_2)
\wedge (\neg y_1 \vee y_2 \vee x_2)
\wedge (y_1 \vee \neg y_2 \vee x_2) \\
&\wedge (\neg y_2 \vee y_3 \vee y_4)
\wedge (y_2 \vee \neg y_3 \vee \neg y_4)
\wedge (y_2 \vee \neg y_3 \vee y_4)
\wedge (y_2 \vee y_3 \vee \neg y_4) \\
&\wedge (\neg y_3 \vee \neg x_1 \vee x_2)
\wedge (y_3 \vee \neg x_1 \vee \neg x_2)
\wedge (y_3 \vee x_1 \vee \neg x_2)
\wedge (y_3 \vee x_1 \vee x_2) \\
&\wedge (\neg y_4 \vee \neg y_5 \vee p)
\wedge (\neg y_4 \vee \neg y_5 \vee \neg p)
\wedge (y_4 \vee y_5 \vee p)
\wedge (y_4 \vee y_5 \vee \neg p) \\
&\wedge (\neg y_5 \vee y_6 \vee x_4)
\wedge (y_5 \vee \neg y_6 \vee \neg x_4)
\wedge (y_5 \vee \neg y_6 \vee x_4)
\wedge (y_5 \vee y_6 \vee \neg x_4) \\
&\wedge (\neg y_6 \vee \neg x_1 \vee \neg x_3)
\wedge (\neg y_6 \vee x_1 \vee x_3)
\wedge (y_6 \vee \neg x_1 \vee x_3)
\wedge (y_6 \vee x_1 \vee \neg x_3).
\end{aligned}
$$

## 34.4-3

> Professor Jagger proposes to show that $\text{SAT} \le_\text P \text{3-CNF-SAT}$ by using only the truth-table technique in the proof of Theorem 34.10, and not the other steps. That is, the professor proposes to take the boolean formula $\phi$, form a truth table for its variables, derive from the truth table a formula in $\text{3-DNF}$ that is equivalent to $\neg\phi$, and then negate and apply DeMorgan's laws to produce a $\text{3-CNF}$ formula equivalent to $\phi$. Show that this strategy does not yield a polynomial-time reduction.

If $\phi$ has $n$ variables, its truth table has $2^n$ rows. In the worst case, $\neg \phi$ is true on $\Theta(2^n)$ rows, so the derived $\text{3-DNF}$ formula has $\Theta(2^n)$ terms. Negating it then gives a $\text{3-CNF}$ formula of exponential size.

Since the input formula itself may have size only polynomial in $n$, this construction takes exponential time and therefore is not a polynomial-time reduction.

## 34.4-4

> Show that the problem of determining whether a boolean formula is a tautology is complete for $\text{co-NP}$. ($\textit{Hint:}$ See Exercise 34.3-7.)

Let $\text{TAUT}$ be the language of tautologies. Its complement consists of formulas that are false under at least one assignment, so $\overline{\text{TAUT}} \in \text{NP}$: a certificate is an assignment making the formula evaluate to $0$.

Also, $\text{SAT} \le_\text P \overline{\text{TAUT}}$ by mapping $\phi$ to $\neg \phi$, since
$$
\phi \in \text{SAT} \iff \neg \phi \notin \text{TAUT}.
$$
Thus $\overline{\text{TAUT}}$ is $\text{NP}$-complete, and by Exercise 34.3-7, $\text{TAUT}$ is complete for $\text{co-NP}$.

## 34.4-5

> Show that the problem of determining the satisfiability of boolean formulas in disjunctive normal form is polynomial-time solvable.

A DNF formula is satisfiable iff at least one of its terms is satisfiable. A term is satisfiable iff it does not contain both $x$ and $\neg x$ for the same variable.

So we scan each term and check whether some variable appears in both signs. If we find a term with no contradiction, accept; otherwise reject. This takes time polynomial in the size of the formula.

## 34.4-6

> Suppose that someone gives you a polynomial-time algorithm to decide formula satisfiability. Describe how to use this algorithm to find satisfying assignments in polynomial time.

First run the satisfiability algorithm on $\phi$. If it says unsatisfiable, stop.

Otherwise, process the variables one by one. For variable $x_i$, substitute all previously fixed values and ask whether the resulting formula is satisfiable with $x_i = 0$. If yes, set $x_i = 0$; otherwise set $x_i = 1$.

Each step makes one polynomial-time call, and there are at most $n$ variables, so the whole assignment is found in polynomial time.

## 34.4-7

> Let $\text{2-CNF-SAT}$ be the set of satisfiable boolean formulas in $\text{CNF}$ with exactly $2$ literals per clause. Show that $\text{2-CNF-SAT} \in P$. Make your algorithm as efficient as possible. ($\textit{Hint:}$ Observe that $x \vee y$ is equivalent to $\neg x \to y$. Reduce $\text{2-CNF-SAT}$ to an efficiently solvable problem on a directed graph.)

Build the implication graph whose vertices are all literals $x_i,\neg x_i$. For each clause $(a \vee b)$, add the two implications
$$
\neg a \to b
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\neg b \to a.
$$

Then the formula is satisfiable iff, for every variable $x_i$, the two literals $x_i$ and $\neg x_i$ are not in the same strongly connected component. If they were in the same SCC, each would imply the other, forcing both to be true.

Thus we can solve $\text{2-CNF-SAT}$ by computing SCCs of the implication graph, which takes linear time in the graph size, i.e. $O(n+m)$ for $n$ variables and $m$ clauses.
